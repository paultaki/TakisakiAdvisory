Strategy That Sticks: Why Most Plans Fail and How to Make Yours Actually Work

April 12, 2025
Strategy, Execution
5 min read
0 Comments
Strategy Leadership Execution Governance

Most strategies fail for one of three reasons:

  • The goal isn’t bold enough
  • No one decides where not to play
  • There’s zero governance to support it

Set a Goal That Actually Stretches You

Toyota once set a goal to double sales of the Camry. They did it—but still weren’t dominating the market. Then they changed the goal: become the top-selling midsize sedan in America. That shift in ambition changed everything. Bold strategy demands a bold target.

Say No—Clearly and on Purpose

Most teams hate this part. It feels limiting. But deciding where not to play is where strategy gets sharp. Gain, the detergent brand, spent years as a "me too" product. Nothing worked—until they made a call: target scent-seekers, and ignore the rest. That clarity turned Gain into a billion-dollar business. You can’t be everything to everyone. Strategy that works starts with the critical few.

The Governance Gap

Even the best strategy crumbles without systems to support it. I use the 5-part “Playing to Win” framework as my base:

  1. What’s our winning aspiration?
  2. Where will we play? (And where won't we?)
  3. How will we win? (The meat of the strategy)
  4. What capabilities do we need?
  5. What governance systems must support this?

Rollout Matters More Than the Slide Deck

If strategy is created in a boardroom and handed down like commandments, it’s dead on arrival. You want buy-in? You’ve got to build with, not for.

When I was three layers removed from the field, I’d build strategy with my directors. They’d bring it to their managers. Managers would take it to their reps and build tactical versions that aligned with the bigger vision but owned the ground-level execution.

The key? Every level needs to leave space for the next to shape it. That’s how it becomes theirs. When the team builds it, they believe in it. And when they believe in it, they chase it hard.

Case in Point: 10,000 Adds in One Month

We ran a promotion with crazy good offers—record-breaking month-level stuff. But the first time we missed. Why?

We targeted the wrong people. We pitched admins managing the phones instead of the CFOs and controllers who actually cared about cost savings. Rookie move.

Still, we hit 9.5K net adds. Not bad—especially considering the previous record was 7.3K. But we weren’t trying to beat the old record. We were trying to crush it. Our goal was 10K+. And we missed.

The next time, we adjusted everything: better targeting, tighter feedback loops, daily check-ins. We hit 10.2K and shattered the territory record.

The Secret Weapon: Team Influencers

Most teams have that one rep others look to before deciding if they’ll follow the new plan. The high performer. The vet. The unofficial voice of the team.

I pull those people in early—right after the director-level strategy session, before the frontline rollout. I ask for input, integrate what makes sense, and then let them know:

"You’re a leader here. People watch what you do. Can I count on your help to bring this to life?"

They’re honored. They’re activated. They become internal champions. That’s how you turn the ship overnight.

Final Thought

Strategy doesn’t fail because it’s flawed. It fails because no one believes in it, no one owns it, and no one builds the systems to support it.

But when you do all three? It sticks. And you win.

Why I Left My Fortune 50 VP Role

The real story of trading titles for transformation

The email came on a Thursday. Another reorg. Another "exciting opportunity" to uproot everything.

I was sitting in my Basking Ridge office, looking at a view that cost Verizon probably $10K a month, and all I could think was: "I can't do this dance anymore."

Mall kiosk to AVP. That was my story. Started in 2003 at Northtown Mall in Spokane, WA, you know, back when people actually went to malls to buy phones? Ended up running B2B operations across multiple states, managing a P&L bigger than most companies' entire revenue.

12 Promotions
4 President's Cabinet Wins

$1B+ Revenue Managed

March 2022. Regional leadership meeting. Someone was presenting on "synergies" and "optimization" and I watched this VP—a brilliant woman, MIT grad—actually shrink herself to keep the GM's ego intact.

That was when I realized:

In twenty years, what am I going to remember? The PowerPoint? The baseball game I'm currently missing for this meeting?

The Receipts

Scottsdale. Dallas. Basking Ridge. Kansas City. Tampa. Seattle. Six moves, six "great opportunities", six times I literally watched my wife rebuild her entire life from zero.

My daughters 8th birthday? Conference call with APAC.
Christmas morning 2019? Fixing a P1 escalation.
My sons first words? Heard them over FaceTime from a hotel in Houston.

But hey, four years of the President's Cabinet? They even gave me a fancy crystal trophy. It might be in a box somewhere.

The thing nobody tells you about executive life: you stop being Paul, and you become, Takisaki from Corporate. Your calendar controls your life. Your phone controls your attention. And people around you start to treat you like a title, not a person.

I can totally recall this moment—Phoenix, 2021—I was at some crappy leadership dinner, and some vendor is laughing entirely too hard at just an average joke I made. And then I synched and thought: no one has been real with me for years, everyone wants something.

The Tuesday I Quit

May 24, 2023. I'm sitting in my home office in Spokane (move #6) while my boss explains to me why I need to move again. This time to Virginia. "Great exposure," he says. "Fast track to SVP".

My wife is in the doorway. She doesn't say anything, but I can see it. That look. The "I support you, but please don't ask me to do this again" look.

I hung up. Opened my laptop. Typed nine words. "I need to discuss my transition from the company."

My retirement lasted 17 days - exactly. It turns out, after running at 100 mph for 20 years, when you stop dead in your tracks, it makes you crazy. I reorganized the garage twice. I built a deck that I didn't need. I drove my family crazy.

Then Jake Morrison called. An old colleague - struggling with his new VP role. "Just grab coffee," he said. Three hours later, I'd mapped out his entire first 90 days on a napkin. He tried to pay me. That's when I realized - this is what I'm supposed to be doing.

Not the Verizon way. Not the corporate way. But real talk for real leaders who are tired of the how.

Why Executive Coaching is Mostly BS

I tried coaching once, back in 2018. It was assigned by my company. The coach had a degree in psychology and zero experience or accountability in P&L and was talking to me about "emotional intelligence," while I was responsible for managing a billion-dollar territory. It lasted for the equivalent of two sessions.

Here's what I do instead:

No Homework, No Workbooks

You are already up to your ears in work. I'm not going to create more work for you. We look at (and fix) real problems together in real time. What's one of your goals, the promotion you are going for? Let's role play the conversation. What about your horrible direct report? Here's exactly what you are going to say and do in Tuesday's meeting.

The 10pm Test

If you cannot text me at 10pm before your board meeting, we weren't doing it right. I've been in the room. I know what they mean when they ask about "strategic alignment." Let's prepare you for that.

Results You Can Bank

One client landed Chief Revenue Officer after being passed over twice. Another client negotiated a $400K exit package instead of getting pushed out. A third saved their marriage. And the last one is more important than the money.

The Weekly Call I Receive

It usually starts off with a line like, "I saw your LinkedIn post..." But what they really mean is: "I'm dying inside and can't tell anyone."

Last week? CMO of a unicorn. Crushing every last metric. Hasn't seen her kids awake in two weeks. Crying in her Tesla in the parking garage.

A month before? PE-backed CEO. $5M comp package. Popping Xanax before board meetings. His wife is already talking to lawyers.

And they are not weak people. They're extraordinary leaders caught inside a game where admitting you're human means career suicide.

They call me because I've sat in their chair. I've felt their pressure. I've made their impossible choices. And I've come out the other side with something that looks like a life.

The Cost of Freedom

Verizon doesn't let AVPs leave easily. There's a retention offer. Guilt-trips. "Think about the team." The full playbook.

My dad - immigrant, worked three jobs - couldn't figure it out. "You're going to quit stability to do what? Coaching?!" And he said it like I was going to be joining a pyramid scheme.

RSUs I left on the table? About $800K.
Pension I walked away from? Don't ask.
Nights I wondered if I had lost my mind? Plenty.

But you know what? My son actually knows what I do for work now. Really knows. Not just "Dad does business stuff." And when he had to have a tough conversation with his coach last week he asked me to practice with him. Not Google. Me.

That's worth more than any stock grant.

I know this may sound like yet another "executive finds meaning" story (LinkedIn's full of them), typically accompanied by some $97 course.

But I'm not selling you transformation in 7 easy steps. I'm offering you something messier: a guide who has been where you're going, who has made the mistakes, who knows what it costs to choose differently.

My clients don't need inspiration. They can get inspiration from TED Talks. They need someone who understands why they are checking email at their child's recital. Who understands that they carry the weight of 1,000 people's livelihoods. Who understands why "just delegate more" is a useless suggestion at their level.

If you're that person, and you're successful by every measure, except the ones you care about, let's talk.

Not because I have answers, but because I have learned the questions that are worth destroying everything to answer.

And sometimes, that is what's needed.

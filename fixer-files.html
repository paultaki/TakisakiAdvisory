From Flawed to Fair: National Quota Model Transformation

When I inherited a broken quota system that punished top performers and rewarded underperformers, I designed a revolutionary four-dimensional model that balanced fairness, business needs, and psychological motivation. The model was so successful that it became Verizon's national standard for all B2B sales representatives.

Consecutive Months of YOY Growth
6+

0

"Paul's quota redesign was a game-changer. It aligned incentives with potential and gave my team clarity on what success looked like. Most importantly, it felt fair."

The Solution

I set out to build a quota model that balanced potential, effort, and performance while removing excuses. After analyzing data across my seven-state region—territory size, customer density, historical results, and rep feedback—I landed on a four-part framework:

  1. Territory Potential (40%) - Divided territories into five zones based on opportunity: market maturity, industry presence, and growth trends.
  2. Zip Code Complexity (10%) - Accounted for geographic sprawl. More zip codes meant more travel time.
  3. Active Lines / Existing Customer Base (30%) - Recognized that more current customers meant more upsell and cross-sell opportunities.
  4. Performance Influence (20%) - Allowed past performance to shape quotas, but with guardrails.

This wasn't just a spreadsheet tweak—it was a behavioral reset. The model aimed to keep the top 20% motivated and unleashed, give underperformers a fair shot without escape hatches, and align quotas with business priorities, not just past results.

A model this nuanced wouldn't stick without clear, consistent communication. Messaging became as critical as the math...

The Unscalable Advantage: Building Deep Connection in a 400-Person Org

In an era of dashboards and KPIs, I maintained an "unscalable" commitment to personal connection—holding regular 1:1s with people three layers down in my 400-person organization. The results speak for themselves.

Leadership at scale is a trap. The bigger things get, the easier it is to hide behind dashboards, reports, and filtered feedback. You get polished presentations. You get secondhand updates. You get just enough truth to feel informed—but not enough to feel connected.

"The most valuable information in your organization rarely makes it up the chain of command unless you intentionally create pathways for it."

The Approach

I developed a systematic approach to making the "unscalable" manageable:

  1. Structured Randomization - A quarterly rotation system ensuring wide and representative coverage
  2. Strategic Question Framework - Four key questions that revealed organizational blind spots

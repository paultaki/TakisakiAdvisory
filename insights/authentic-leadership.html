Authentic Leadership in Action: My Playbook for Building Trust and Driving Results

May 15, 2025 • 6 min read

Real leadership isn't about titles—it's about authenticity, connection, and creating an environment where people genuinely want to follow you. Here's how servant leadership builds trust and drives extraordinary results.

Paul Takisaki
Executive Coach & Leadership Strategist

I once reported to a CRO at a Fortune 15 company whose emotional intelligence set her apart. Every call began with a question about my kids—strangers to her. When I mentioned my son's chess match, she shared tales of her boys' tournaments, adding, "get him into sports too, chess alone can make kids quirky." Her wit cut through the C-suite veneer, making her feel like a peer. Her texts mixed work, humor, and heart, building real connection.

She arrived admitting she didn't have all the answers and relied on us frontline leaders to learn. Half her time was spent in the field—roundtables, hallway chats, customer meetings—asking tough questions and fiercely defending her teams. Her intellect was surgical; she could distill complex debates into five ranked points on the fly, recalling them verbatim weeks later. Under her, our business soared, not despite her intensity, but because she fought alongside us. Her leadership didn't shape me—I was already wired for authenticity—but it reinforced how it feels to have a leader who gets it.

Here's how I put servant leadership into practice to drive trust and results.

The Playbook: Authentic Leadership in Action

1. Build Trust Through Candor

In a new role, I meet one-on-one with as many team members as possible in my first 90 days—everyone, if the team's 150 or smaller. I read their vibe: Nervous? Excited? I open with vulnerability to level the playing field.

My pitch: "I'm Paul. Thanks for this—I know it might feel like a summons, but I'm genuinely grateful. I'm not big on titles. The best ideas come from the frontlines, so I'm here to listen. No prep needed. I've been here 20 years, married 20, got three kids who run me ragged, and two dogs who ignore me. Tell me about you."

This dismantles hierarchy. I've been a rep; I know their grind. During a voluntary separation period, I shared my own unease: "I'm wrestling with the same questions. Is my job safe? My team's? I've been here forever—leaving feels like a leap." This honesty builds trust. Reps say, "You get it," knowing I won't push pointless initiatives.

"People will forget what you said, but never how you made them feel."

2. Foster a Culture of Care

I take detailed notes—kids' names, pets, where they met their spouse. These go into a spreadsheet and contact cards for quick recall. My aunt, a former JPMorgan Chase chairman, taught me this: treat everyone, from janitor to CEO, with equal respect. She jotted down personal details to reference later, and I've made it my own. People beam when I ask about their daughter's college or their cat's quirks. As Maya Angelou put it, "People will forget what you said, but never how you made them feel."

Before visiting remote offices, I review my notes on the flight. Walking in, I pick up where we left off: "How's your son's soccer going?" The shock—"You remembered?"—is pure gold. It shows I'm not just their boss; I'm invested.

3. Lead with Purpose, Deliver Results

Drawing from John Mackey's Whole Foods philosophy, I lead with purpose—seeing people at their best. This isn't soft; it's strategic. Employee satisfaction drives customer experience and growth, as Chick-fil-A proves with industry-leading retention despite closing Sundays. My teams shattered records in every territory I led, not for metrics, but because they felt valued. They worked for me, not just the company.

Skeptics might call this approach touchy-feely, unscalable in cutthroat markets. Yet Chick-fil-A's model thrives in a brutal industry, and my teams' loyalty and results speak for themselves. Authenticity isn't a luxury; it's a competitive edge.

Leadership Insight

In a world of corporate masks and strategic distance, genuine connection becomes your most powerful differentiator. It's not just about being nice—it's about creating the psychological safety that unleashes peak performance.

The Bottom Line

When I retired from my company, my team made a farewell video—not for my KPIs, but for how I made them feel: seen and respected. They didn't thank me for boosting sales by 25% year-over-year—they thanked me for knowing their kids' names, understanding their fears, and making work feel meaningful. In a sea of corporate noise, authenticity wins. Lead from the trenches, because that's where the real work happens.

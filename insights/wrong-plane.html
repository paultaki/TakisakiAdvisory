You're On The Wrong Plane

May 25, 2025 • 6 min read

The real reason your high performers quit isn't money or title—it's misdirection. Here's why your stars leave when they realize they're on the wrong plane.

The Misdirection Problem

The departure email always says something diplomatic.

"After much thought, I've decided to pursue a new opportunity..."

But the real exit interview—the raw, unfiltered one that happens at the bar after someone's left—tells a different story:

"I realized I'd been on the wrong plane for two years."

This metaphor keeps surfacing when high performers explain their exits. And after coaching hundreds of leaders through their transitions, I've found it's rarely about compensation, titles, or even toxic environments—though those make convenient explanations.

The core issue is misdirection—when someone's contributions are consistently directed toward outcomes that don't align with their strengths, values, or career aspirations.

The "Wrong Plane" Moment

Here's how it typically unfolds:

  • A promising professional joins your company excited about destination X
  • Due to shifting priorities, restructuring or poor expectation-setting, they spend 18+ months working toward destination Y
  • They excel anyway (because great people make the best of situations)
  • One day, they have a "wait, what am I doing?" moment of clarity
  • Within 3-6 months, they're gone—usually to a role that gets them back on the original trajectory

What makes this particularly dangerous is that high performers are masters of adaptation. They'll build relevant skills, deliver impressive results, and show every sign of engagement—right until they don't.

The revelation often arrives suddenly: "I'm two years into a journey toward something I never wanted in the first place."

Why Retention "Solutions" Usually Fail

When organizations notice flight risk in a key player, the playbook is predictable:

  1. Offer more money
  2. Create a new title
  3. Promise future opportunities

But none of these address the core problem. As one senior director told me after rejecting a substantial counter-offer:

"They offered me a 30% raise to keep building a function I have zero passion for. That's just a more expensive prison."

This isn't about being ungrateful or entitled. It's about the profound human need for meaningful contribution—to feel that your work matters in ways that align with your values and aspirations.

Diagnosing Flight Risk Before Takeoff

Leaders can identify misdirection long before the resignation letter by looking for these subtle indicators:

  • Skill-Priority Disconnects: When someone's strongest skills aren't relevant to their core deliverables
  • Success Without Satisfaction: High achievement paired with diminishing enthusiasm
  • Increasing Self-Editing: Fewer original ideas, more compliance-focused execution
  • Identity Distancing: Subtle shifts from "we" to "the company" in how they reference work
  • Reduced Investment in Future Systems: Declining interest in longer-term infrastructure or improvement

Note that none of these are performance issues. In fact, most managers mistake these signals as "adjusting to the role" or "maturing in the organization."

Leadership Insight

High performers rarely leave companies. They leave misalignments between what energizes them and what you're asking them to build.

Redirecting Mid-Flight

The window for meaningful intervention is narrower than most leaders realize. Once someone has their "wrong plane" epiphany, the countdown begins—and it's usually measured in weeks, not months.

Effective redirection requires radical honesty from both sides:

  • Ask directly: "If you could redesign your role to maximize both impact and fulfillment, what would change?"
  • Acknowledge the misdirection without defensiveness: "I realize we've had you focused on X when your strengths and interests are clearly in Y"
  • Explore possibility before feasibility: "Before we discuss constraints, what would the ideal look like?"
  • Make concrete changes within 30 days—token adjustments only accelerate departure

Most importantly, recognize that sometimes the right answer is helping someone find the right plane—even if it's not at your company. I've seen leaders who facilitate transitions to better-fit roles elsewhere and earn loyalty that pays dividends for decades.

The Price of "Just Now Realizing"

Organizations pay a staggering price for misdirection, and it extends far beyond the standard 1-2x salary replacement cost estimate.

The real costs include:

  • Abandoned initiatives that lose momentum during transition
  • Institutional knowledge that walks out the door
  • The contagious nature of departures (high performers typically leave in clusters)
  • The opportunity cost of what these people could have built if properly aligned

But perhaps the most significant cost is the story that forms in your culture: that people's highest contributions aren't recognized or valued—that excellence gets rewarded with misdirection rather than meaningful growth.

The solution isn't complicated, but it requires something many leaders find uncomfortable: regular, honest conversation about whether people feel they're on the right plane.

Because if they're having that conversation with themselves, you're already losing them.

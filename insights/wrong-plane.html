Wrong Plane, Right Destination

May 18, 2025 • 5 min read

Ever witnessed someone panic on a flight after realizing they boarded the wrong plane? That's exactly what happened when I took over a technical B2B sales team—except 70% of my staff stood up in panic, and it was entirely by design.

The In-Flight Realization

The team I inherited was technically proficient—IT backgrounds, intimate knowledge of gigahertz, megabytes, serial ports, and split tunneling capabilities. They could recite spec sheets flawlessly, putting customers to sleep with technical minutiae. But sales were plummeting for a clear reason: this commissioned team didn't know how to sell.

"Sales were in the slumps, and it didn't take long to realize why: instead of assisting B2B sales reps push our more cutting-edge technical solutions, they were acting like the rep's personal Google when they needed a spec sheet."

The Course Correction

Instead of functioning as glorified Google searches for product specifications, what they needed to do was:

  • Lead customer meetings from the front
  • Uncover pain points through strategic questioning
  • Connect value propositions directly to business challenges
  • Drive toward asking for the sale
  • Model this process so reps could replicate it independently

So I changed our destination. Over the metaphorical intercom, I announced: "We will be experts in the value proposition of our tech solutions that meet identified and targeted business needs and drive a clear ROI."

I asked how they thought we would accomplish this. The response?

Crickets.

The New Flight Plan

I broke it down word by word:

  1. Experts in value proposition - Understanding customer pain points through questioning
  2. Demonstrating solutions - Showing precisely how we solve specific issues
  3. Driving ROI - Articulating clear business value in financial terms

When it clicked...this was the face of 70% of my team that day.

Technical knowledge would take a back seat until we mastered the sales process completely—becoming the gold standard in consultative selling.

That's when 70% realized they had boarded the wrong plane. Most returned to operational IT roles where they could focus on their technical strengths. They were happier, and we quickly filled positions with people aligned to our new direction.

Executing the Strategy

The transformation wasn't overnight. We:

Preparation Phase

  • Invested weeks in targeted sales training
  • Conducted extensive role-playing sessions
  • Built structured content for consistent execution

Execution Phase

  • Implemented pre-meeting strategy sessions
  • Conducted post-meeting reviews with lessons learned
  • Maintained disciplined meeting agendas and sales fundamentals

The Results Speak

250%
First year growth (YoY)
550%
Second year additional growth

The company held a national role-play competition where sales teams demonstrated their skills, with winners advancing to perform for senior executives and attend a private concert with a major artist. One of my solutions architects competed directly against sales teams, advancing to the finals—proving we could maintain technical competence while excelling at sales fundamentals.

The Crucial Lesson

Customers don't buy because a device has seven serial ports or the latest processor. They buy to solve problems or access opportunities previously unavailable.

"You must be crystal clear about direction, or you'll end up with team members expecting to relax on a Florida beach when you're planning to climb Everest. They'll be unprepared and hold everyone back."

Had I tried to transform the 70% who needed to leave through other means, the change would have been too slow and created unnecessary resentment.

"They didn't need more training. They needed a destination."

The Key Takeaway

This became my first lesson in the power of a clear mission statement: When everyone understands the destination, those who belong will stay, and those who don't will find their right plane.

THE UNSCALABLE ADVANTAGE

Why I still held 1:1s with everyone, 3 layers down

You can't outsource being known.

Paul Takisaki
Executive Coach & Leadership Strategist

I ran a 400+ person org.

Sr. Directors, Directors, Managers. Solutions Architects, Product Specialists, Engineers, Analysts, etc. Coast-to-coast. All eyes on performance.

But I still made time for 1:1s with people three layers down. Not because I had the time. Because I had a responsibility.

The Scalability Trap

Leadership at scale is a trap. The bigger things get, the easier it is to hide behind dashboards, reports, and filtered feedback.

You get polished presentations. You get secondhand updates. You get just enough truth to feel informed—but not enough to feel connected.

That's why I kept showing up. In market. In the field. On calls. I wanted to hear the stories, not just the stats.

My calendar was perpetually overbooked. My exec peers thought I was wasting time. "Delegate that," they'd say. "You can't scale yourself." They weren't wrong about the math. Four hundred people at 30 minutes each is 200 hours. There's no way to make that add up.

But here's what they missed: I wasn't trying to hold 400 1:1s. I was strategically connecting with people at all levels to maintain real connection to the frontline. The goal wasn't checking a box for everyone—it was preventing the isolation that happens to leaders who only talk to other leaders.

When you only speak to people at your level, you only hear what people at your level want you to hear.

The Story That Changed Everything

I remember sitting with a frontline rep who thought he was on his way out. Burnt out. Overlooked. Half in.

All I did was listen.

Two quarters later, he was one of our top performers. Not because I gave him a new playbook. Because someone finally gave a damn.

That's the unscalable advantage.

In a world obsessed with automation, AI, and efficiency, genuine human connection becomes the rarest resource. The executives who maintain it have an edge that no algorithm can match.

How to Scale the Unscalable

I'm not suggesting every leader can or should try to meet with hundreds of people. But there are practical ways to maintain connection without burning out:

01

Meetings with Purpose

Structure skip-level 1:1s with consistent questions to identify patterns, not just solve individual problems. Track themes in a system that helps you see the whole picture.

02

Structured Randomization

Each quarter, meet with a strategic mix: high-potentials identified by managers, completely random selections, problem-solvers, and those expressing concerns.

03

Making the Most of Travel

When visiting regional offices, block half a day for consecutive 15-minute conversations with frontline team members—informal check-ins that yield invaluable insights.

04

Digital Openness

Maintain an "Ask Me Anything" digital channel for anonymous questions. Answer them monthly in a video everyone can access, creating transparency and surfacing hidden issues.

Presence in Practice

I used a standard set of questions that let me spot patterns across the organization:

  • "What's one policy or process that makes your job harder than it needs to be?"
  • "If you were in my position for a day, what's the first thing you'd change?"
  • "What do customers tell you that leadership never hears?"
  • "What's something we used to do well that we've stopped doing?"

Then I sat in the cubicles—not the conference rooms. When I visited remote offices, I wouldn't announce it. I'd set up shop in an empty desk and plug in my laptop. No entourage. No title. Just another teammate.

They were shocked at first. But as soon as they saw I wasn't there to micromanage or judge, they started talking. Some days I wanted earmuffs—what they said was brutally honest. But I didn't flinch, didn't gossip, and didn't break trust. That's why they kept telling me the truth.

The most valuable information in your organization rarely makes it up the chain of command unless you intentionally create pathways for it.

Culture Lives in the In-Between Moments

Culture doesn't live in strategy decks. It lives in the in-between moments:

A real check-in when someone's off.

A note when someone crushes it.

A conversation that says, "I see you."

The higher you go, the more dangerous it gets to forget that.

I'd rather build something a little slower, a little messier—if it means the people inside it actually want to stay.

Because loyalty isn't built by perks. It's built by presence.

And presence doesn't scale. But it sticks.

Leadership Philosophy

Signature tools that delivered consistent results throughout my career

The Handlebar Test

Let go. If your team swerves, you've got control, not leadership. True leadership emerges when you trust your team to move forward without your constant intervention. Just like riding a bike without hands, the stability comes after you release control.

Learn More

The 80/20 Rule

Focus on the vital few priorities that drive 80% of results. I identified the 3-4 metrics that truly drove business value and ruthlessly prioritized them. This approach not only delivered exceptional results but allowed for sustainable work-life balance.

Learn More

Failure Fridays

"If you're not failing, you're not trying." We celebrated what didn't work in weekly sessions where team members volunteered to share recent failures and what they learned. This practice transformed our innovation culture and boosted creativity.

Learn More

Aggressive Agreement

Conflict where you're on the same side but still misfiring. This framework helps teams express passionate disagreement without damaging relationships, fostering both innovation and cohesion when tackling complex challenges.

The Leadership Flywheel

Throughout my career, I often felt like an outsider. While other leaders were putting in conspicuous 80-hour weeks and managing through force of personality, I built strategy and stayed focused on what truly mattered. I wasn't paying attention to the 80% of noise that consumed my peers' attention.

The difference? I wasn't on a treadmill. I was building a flywheel.

While many leaders sprint from day one, pushing performance through force and adrenaline, I focused on building long-term systems. My flywheel turned slowly at first—when people questioned my impact, my urgency, even my hire. This early phase is the hardest. But I knew what I was building.

Over time, the flywheel gained momentum. My teams didn't just hit goals—they became self-driven. They solved problems before I saw them. By the time my peers were burning out trying to hold their systems together, mine was running without me. I had worked myself out of the job—by design.

You can get quick wins by hammering every nail yourself. But that only works if you're willing to swing forever. I wasn't. I was building something that would keep spinning long after I let go.
